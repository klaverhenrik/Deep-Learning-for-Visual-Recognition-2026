## How to use this session

Come with a specific task. Groups that arrive without a plan
tend to leave without having accomplished much.

**Suggested priorities, roughly in order:**

1. **Run any missing experiments.**
   If your results table has gaps — ablations you planned but
   did not run, a comparison you wanted to make — do them now.
   After today there is no more supervised GPU time.

2. **Finalise your results section.**
   Every table and figure should be in its final form.
   Check against the Week 11 guide — axis labels, captions,
   consistent metric, bold best result.

3. **Write up your analysis.**
   The discussion of your results is often the hardest part
   to write and the part most students leave too late.
   What did you learn? Where does your model fail? What would
   you try next if you had more time?

4. **Check your code submission.**
   Your code should run from top to bottom without errors
   on a fresh Colab session. Test this before submitting.
   Include a README with instructions for running the code.

5. **Proofread.**
   Read your report out loud. If a sentence does not make sense
   when spoken, it will not make sense when read either.

## A note on scope

If you are discovering major gaps in your work today, focus on
what you can do well rather than trying to do everything.

A report that analyses three experiments thoroughly is better
than one that describes ten experiments superficially.
A results section with honest failure analysis is better than
one that only shows successes.

Quality over quantity.

## The video presentation

Your final submission includes a **5-minute group video presentation**.
This is part of the graded hand-in — not optional.

### What to include

A 5-minute video covering roughly:

| Time | Content |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:30 | Problem statement — what you worked on and why it is interesting |
| 0:30–1:30 | Dataset — show some images, describe the challenge |
| 1:30–3:00 | Approach and experiments — what you tried and what happened |
| 3:00–4:00 | Results — your best result, confusion matrix or key figure |
| 4:00–5:00 | Discussion — what worked, what did not, what you would try next |

### Practical tips

**Record with screen sharing.** Open your notebook or report, share your
screen, and talk through it. You do not need slides.

**Show actual outputs.** Generated images, detection results, segmentation
masks, loss curves — visual outputs are more compelling than text.

**Each group member should speak.** The video is group-level but all
members should contribute visibly.

**Keep it under 5 minutes.** Going over the limit signals poor preparation.

**Do not read from the report.** Talk naturally about what you did.
The video should feel like you are explaining your work to a colleague,
not presenting it formally.

### Recording tools

- **Loom** (free) — screen recording with webcam overlay
- **OBS Studio** (free) — more control, slightly more setup
- **Google Meet / Zoom** — record a call where you share your screen
- **PowerPoint / Keynote** — built-in recording if you prefer slides
